A Social Return on Investment

For every $1 invested in the YEP program there is a societal benefit of $27.

Key Highlights

Over 1,000 participants benefit yearly, across 5 countries: Canada, Spain, Poland, Ethiopia, and Niger

YEP is one of only a handful of organizations to receive the United Nations’ Innovation award

Over 300,000 volunteers hours contributed by youth, a value of $4.5 million

The investment in each youth is $250, compared to the industry average of $3,740

What is Social Return On Investment (SROI)?

Social Return on Investment (SROI) is a concept and practice pioneered by the Roberts Enterprise Development Fund in 1999, that “provides a metric for quantifying nonprofits’ financial and social values…”

Example 1

The Institute for Social Research and the School of Public Health at the University of Michigan evaluated the Boys & Girls Clubs of America. It found that Boys & Girls Clubs produce lasting value for youth, families and communities, through improved grades, reduced alcohol use, increased earning power of parents and of youth when they become adults, and reduce expenditures in the criminal justice system. Based on expenditures to achieve outcomes, it was found that every for every $1 invested in the organization, a return of $9.60 social impact was generated.

Example 2

The International SROI Network applied SROI to an example of creating an emergency medical system in Kenya. Costs included ambulances, paramedics and equipment. Benefits included value of lives saved, less disability, acquisition of skills through training, and reduced welfare spending. If the Kenyan government had spent $10,000 to create the emergency medical system (costs), and if they no longer had to tackle the issues of disability, welfare, etc. which usually cost $30,000 (benefits), then to calculate SROI we would divide $30,000 by $10,000. This would yield to a ratio of 3:1, indicating that an investment of $1 into the medical system delivers $3 in value to the Kenyan society.

Employment

Higher Academic Achievement among Youth

The YEP Advantage: 85% of YEP youth report higher grades following YEP program participation and their increased ability to perform academically leads them to strive for higher education.

What the research says: Students who achieve higher grades early on are more likely to pursue post-secondary education. The better the academic performance, the more likely they are to aspire to university studies. SROI studies indicate that greater academic achievement among young people leads to a monetary metric value of $13,111 per person.

Improved Language Proficiency among Adults

The YEP Advantage: 100% of adults in the program reported increased language proficiency.

What the research says: Increased language proficiency of adults leads to increase in earning power in the Canadian workplace. The potential increase in mean earnings for those with high language abilities compared to those with lower language proficiencies is $3,600 per worker.

Improved Literacy Among Adults

The YEP Advantage: 100% of adults in the program reported increased digital literacy after participating in a YEP program.

What the research says: Improved digital literacy of adults leads to an increase in earning power. The national productivity gain is $1,600 per adult worker.

Mental Health and Wellbeing

Reduced Feelings of Parentification among Youth

The YEP Advantage: After their parents participate in a YEP program, their children reap the benefit of being able to spend less time helping them in everyday tasks. 1 in 4 youth in YEP live in parentified households.

What the research says: Youth who are not parentified earn $7,428/year more than those who experience parentification.

Higher Self-Esteem among Youth

The YEP Advantage: YEP youth report improved feelings of accomplishment and personal development after completing the program.

What the research says: Children from low-income families with higher self-esteem during adolescence earn $7,000/year more compared to those with poor self-esteem.

Language Proficiency and Reduced Isolation among Adults

The YEP Advantage: 1 in 4 adults and seniors in enrolled in YEP are learning communication skills in our national language, and reducing their anxiety and social isolation.

What the research says: Ability to effectively communicate the national language reduces immigrant social anxiety and reduces social isolation, resulting in reduced government spending (e.g. direct and indirect costs to government for anxiety disorders in Canada). The Canadian government spends $7,428 per person who suffers from anxiety (direct and indirect costs) on anxiety disorders each year.

Better Communities

Increased Likelihood of Volunteering Again among Youth

The YEP Advantage: More than 80% of YEP youth cite that they would volunteer again in the future. YEP youth learn and reap the benefits of volunteering, and they report increased likelihood of volunteering again in the future.

What the research says: There are future community benefits via increased likelihood to volunteer as adult, such as increased educational and occupational opportunities, as well as mental and physical health benefits. Moreover, the value of volunteerism to the community is $3,599.14.

More Efficient Spending of Donated Funds

The YEP Advantage: YEP mostly operates in at-risk communities. The average cost for other programs to support youth at-risk is $3740 per youth. The cost YEP spends per youth is $250.

What the research says: At-risk volunteers increase their likelihood of becoming better role models in the community. 13% of children who do not participate in volunteer activities are likely to partake in delinquent behaviour compared to only 7% of children who participated in volunteer activities
